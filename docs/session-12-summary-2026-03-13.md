## 🎯 Content Additions

### 1. Criminal Psychology (psy3c1)

**Biological Theories**:
- Genetic factors (twin studies, adoption studies, MAOA 'warrior gene', XYY syndrome)
- Brain abnormalities (prefrontal cortex, amygdala, limbic system, Raine et al. 1997)
- Neurotransmitters (serotonin, dopamine, noradrenaline, testosterone)
- Evaluation (strengths: scientific evidence; limitations: reductionist, deterministic, correlation ≠ causation)

**Psychodynamic Explanations**:
- Freud's theory (weak/overactive/deviant superego)
- Bowlby's maternal deprivation theory (affectionless psychopathy, 44 juvenile thieves study)
- Evaluation (strengths: early childhood emphasis; limitations: unfalsifiable, deterministic, gender bias)

**Behavioral Explanations**:
- Operant conditioning (reinforcement/punishment, differential reinforcement)
- Social learning theory (Bandura, observation, vicarious reinforcement, media violence)
- Evaluation (strengths: scientific, environmental factors; limitations: reductionist, deterministic)

**Cognitive Explanations**:
- Moral reasoning (Kohlberg's stages, Palmer & Hollin 1998)
- Cognitive distortions (hostile attribution bias, minimalisation, denial of responsibility, victim blaming)
- Evaluation (strengths: thinking processes, CBT implications; limitations: correlation ≠ causation)

**Eysenck's Personality Theory**:
- Three dimensions (Extraversion, Neuroticism, Psychoticism)
- Criminal personality (High E + High N + High P)
- Evaluation (strengths: scientific, measurable; limitations: inconsistent evidence, self-report bias)

**Treatment Approaches**:
- Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) - anger management, 25-30% reduction in reoffending
- Token economy - operant conditioning in prisons
- Restorative justice - victim-offender mediation, 7-14% reduction in reoffending

**Psychology and Legal System**:
- Eyewitness testimony (Loftus & Palmer, weapon focus, cognitive interview)
- Jury decision-making (defendant characteristics, pre-trial publicity, majority influence)

### 2. Health Psychology (psy3c2)

**Health Belief Models**:
- Health Belief Model (HBM) - 6 components (perceived susceptibility, severity, benefits, barriers, cues to action, self-efficacy)
- Theory of Planned Behavior (TPB) - attitude, subjective norm, perceived behavioral control → intention → behavior
- Evaluation (strengths: practical applications; limitations: assumes rationality, intention-behavior gap)

**Stress and Health**:
- Stress definition and types (acute, chronic, eustress, distress)
- General Adaptation Syndrome (Selye) - 3 stages (alarm, resistance, exhaustion)
- Life events (Holmes & Rahe SRRS, death of spouse = 100 LCU)
- Daily hassles (Kanner et al. 1981, better predictor than life events)
- Evaluation of stress research

**Physiological Effects of Stress**:
- Cardiovascular health (hypertension, atherosclerosis, Type A personality)
- Immune system (psychoneuroimmunology, Kiecolt-Glaser studies, Cohen et al. 1991)

**Coping Strategies**:
- Problem-focused coping (planning, active coping, seeking information)
- Emotion-focused coping (emotional support, positive reappraisal, acceptance, denial)
- Social support (emotional, instrumental, informational, appraisal support)
- Evaluation (strengths: practical; limitations: individual differences, maladaptive coping)

**Health Promotion**:
- Fear appeals (moderate fear most effective, Witte & Allen 2000)
- Social norms approach (correct misperceptions, Perkins & Berkowitz 1986)

**Interventions**:
- Smoking cessation (NRT, medication, behavioral therapy, CBT, 25-30% quit rate)
- Weight loss (dietary changes, exercise, behavioral strategies, 5-10% weight loss maintained)
- Exercise promotion (goal-setting, self-monitoring, implementation intentions)
- Adherence to medical treatment (simplify regimen, reminders, education, 20-30% increase)
- Evaluation (strengths: evidence-based; limitations: high relapse rates, social inequalities)

### 3. Debates in Psychology (psy3c3)

**Nature vs. Nurture**:
- Definitions (nature: genes, brain, evolution; nurture: learning, experience, culture)
- Nature evidence (twin studies, adoption studies, intelligence, schizophrenia)
- Nurture evidence (learning theories, cultural differences, feral children)
- Interactionist approach (diathesis-stress model, epigenetics, Caspi et al. 2003)
- Evaluation (nature vs. nurture vs. interactionist)

**Free Will vs. Determinism**:
- Definitions (free will: conscious control; determinism: behavior caused by external factors)
- Types of determinism (biological, environmental, psychic)
- Free will arguments (subjective experience, moral responsibility, therapy effectiveness)
- Determinism arguments (Libet 1983, behaviorism, Freud)
- Evaluation (soft determinism/compatibilism as compromise)

**Reductionism vs. Holism**:
- Definitions (reductionism: break down into components; holism: whole > sum of parts)
- Levels of explanation (sociocultural → psychological → biological → chemical → physical)
- Types of reductionism (biological, environmental, experimental)
- Holism arguments (Gestalt psychology, humanistic psychology, systems theory)
- Evaluation (interactionist approach - use multiple levels)

**Idiographic vs. Nomothetic**:
- Definitions (idiographic: study individuals, qualitative; nomothetic: study groups, quantitative)
- Idiographic examples (case studies: HM, Phineas Gage, Genie; qualitative research)
- Nomothetic examples (experiments: Milgram, Asch; psychometric testing; biological research)
- Evaluation (both needed - idiographic generates ideas, nomothetic tests them)

**Psychology as Science**:
- Scientific criteria (objectivity, empiricism, replicability, falsifiability)
- Theory construction (explains data, testable predictions, parsimonious)
- Paradigms (Kuhn) - psychology is pre-paradigmatic (multiple competing approaches)
- Evaluation (scientific aspects vs. unscientific aspects)

**Applications**:
- Evaluating studies using debates
- Biopsychosocial model (integration of biological, psychological, social factors)
