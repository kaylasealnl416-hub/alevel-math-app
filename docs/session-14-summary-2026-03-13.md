### 1. Introduction to Clinical Psychology (psy4c1)

**Historical Development**:
- Ancient times to modern era (Philippe Pinel, Freud, deinstitutionalization, DSM development)

**Classification Systems**:
- **DSM-5**: American Psychiatric Association, diagnostic criteria, 20+ disorder categories
- **ICD-11**: World Health Organization, global health statistics, cultural sensitivity
- Evaluation (strengths: standardization, communication; limitations: categorical approach, comorbidity, stigma, cultural bias)

**Assessment Methods**:
- Clinical interviews (unstructured, structured, semi-structured)
- Mental Status Examination (MSE) - 11 components
- Psychometric tests (WAIS, MMPI-2, NEO-PI-R, Rorschach, TAT)
- Behavioral observation (naturalistic, controlled, self-monitoring)
- Neuropsychological assessment (Halstead-Reitan, brain imaging)

**Diagnosis Process**:
- 8 steps (referral, initial assessment, gather information, formulation, diagnosis, differential diagnosis, treatment planning, review)

**Prevalence and Incidence**:
- Epidemiology findings (anxiety most common ~18%, women higher depression/anxiety, onset in adolescence)

**Cultural Considerations**:
- Culture-bound syndromes (Taijin kyofusho, Ataque de nervios, Koro)
- Cultural competence in diagnosis

**Reliability and Validity**:
- Inter-rater reliability (Rosenhan 1973 study)
- Types of validity (descriptive, predictive, construct, aetiological)

**Ethical Issues**:
- Confidentiality, informed consent, dual relationships, boundaries, cultural sensitivity, diagnosis ethics

### 2. Anxiety Disorders (psy4c2)

**Types of Anxiety Disorders**:
- **Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD)**: Excessive worry 6+ months, ~3% prevalence
- **Specific Phobias**: 5 types (animal, natural environment, blood-injection-injury, situational, other), ~10% prevalence
- **Social Anxiety Disorder**: Fear of social situations, ~7% prevalence, onset early adolescence
- **Panic Disorder**: Recurrent panic attacks, ~3% prevalence
- **Agoraphobia**: Fear of situations where escape difficult, ~2% prevalence
- **OCD**: Obsessions and compulsions, 1+ hour/day, ~2% prevalence

**Biological Explanations**:
- Genetics (twin studies: MZ 30%, DZ 15%; specific genes: 5-HTTLPR, COMT)
- Neurotransmitters (serotonin, GABA, noradrenaline)
- Brain structures (amygdala overactive, prefrontal cortex underactive, hippocampus smaller in PTSD)

**Psychological Explanations**:
- Classical conditioning (two-process theory: acquisition + maintenance)
- Cognitive biases (attentional bias, interpretation bias, memory bias, catastrophizing)

**Treatments**:
- **CBT**: Cognitive restructuring + behavioral experiments, 60-70% improvement
- **Exposure therapy**: Systematic desensitization, flooding, 70-80% improvement for phobias
- **Medication**: SSRIs (50-60% improvement), benzodiazepines (fast-acting), beta-blockers (performance anxiety)
- **Relaxation techniques**: Progressive muscle relaxation, deep breathing, mindfulness, biofeedback
- Evaluation (CBT vs. medication vs. combination)

### 3. Mood Disorders (psy4c3)

**Major Depressive Disorder (MDD)**:
- Diagnosis (DSM-5: 5+ symptoms for 2+ weeks, including depressed mood or anhedonia)
- Epidemiology (~7% past year, ~17% lifetime, women 2x more likely)
- Subtypes (melancholic, atypical, psychotic, postpartum)

**Bipolar Disorders**:
- **Bipolar I**: At least one manic episode (1+ week, severe impairment)
- **Bipolar II**: At least one hypomanic episode (4+ days, less severe) + major depressive episode
- **Cyclothymic Disorder**: Chronic mood instability 2+ years
- Prevalence (~1% each), high suicide risk (15-20%)

**Biological Explanations**:
- Monoamine hypothesis (serotonin, noradrenaline, dopamine deficiency)
- Genetics (twin studies: MDD MZ 40%, DZ 20%; Bipolar MZ 70%, DZ 20%; gene-environment interaction)
- Brain structures (prefrontal cortex underactive, hippocampus smaller, amygdala overactive)

**Psychological Theories**:
- Beck's cognitive theory (negative triad, negative schemas, cognitive distortions)
- Learned helplessness (Seligman, depressive attributional style)
- Behavioral activation (lack of positive reinforcement → vicious cycle)

**Treatments**:
- **Antidepressants**: SSRIs (50-60% respond), SNRIs, tricyclics, MAOIs
- **ECT**: 70-90% respond, most effective for severe depression
- **CBT**: Cognitive restructuring + behavioral activation, 50-60% respond
- **Other psychotherapies**: IPT, psychodynamic, MBCT
- **Bipolar treatments**: Lithium, anticonvulsants, antipsychotics, psychotherapy

**Suicide Risk**:
- Risk factors (mental disorders, previous attempt, hopelessness, access to means)
- Assessment (ask directly, assess ideation/plan/intent/means)
- Intervention (immediate safety, hospitalization, safety plan, treatment, follow-up)
- Prevention (restrict access to means, screening, crisis hotlines)

### 4. Psychotherapeutic Techniques (psy4c4)

**Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT)**:
- Principles (thoughts-feelings-behaviors interconnected, present-focused, time-limited, collaborative)
- Cognitive techniques (cognitive restructuring, Socratic questioning, thought records, behavioral experiments)
- Behavioral techniques (behavioral activation, graded task assignment, activity scheduling, exposure, relaxation)
- Effectiveness (60-70% improvement for depression/anxiety, as effective as medication, lower relapse)
- Applications (depression, anxiety, PTSD, OCD, eating disorders, insomnia)

**Psychodynamic Therapy**:
- Principles (unconscious conflicts cause problems, insight → change, long-term, focus on past)
- Techniques (free association, dream analysis, interpretation, analysis of resistance/transference)
- Effectiveness (some evidence, useful for complex problems)
- Evaluation (strengths: addresses root causes; limitations: time-consuming, difficult to test, focuses on past)

**Humanistic Therapy**:
- Person-centered therapy (Rogers): Self-actualization, incongruence, non-directive
- Core conditions (unconditional positive regard, empathy, congruence)
- Techniques (active listening, reflect feelings, open-ended questions)
- Effectiveness (modest evidence)
- Evaluation (strengths: empowering, growth-focused; limitations: vague, not for severe disorders, cultural bias)

**Family and Couples Therapy**:
- Family systems theory (homeostasis, identified patient, triangulation, boundaries)
- Techniques (genogram, reframing, structural interventions, communication training)
- Couples therapy (behavioral, emotionally-focused)

**Group Therapy**:
- Types (psychoeducational, support groups, process groups)
- Therapeutic factors (Yalom): Universality, hope, information, altruism, interpersonal learning, cohesion
- Advantages (cost-effective, social support, practice skills)

**Biological Treatments**:
- Psychopharmacology (antidepressants, antipsychotics, mood stabilizers, anxiolytics, stimulants)
- ECT and other somatic treatments (TMS, DBS, VNS)

**Ethical Considerations**:
- Informed consent, confidentiality, boundaries, cultural competence, evidence-based practice, termination

**Evidence-Based Practice**:
- Definition (integrate research + expertise + client values)
- Research hierarchy (RCTs, meta-analyses)
- Empirically supported treatments (ESTs)
- Efficacy vs. effectiveness
- Common factors (therapeutic alliance, empathy, client expectations)
- Treatment matching and integration
