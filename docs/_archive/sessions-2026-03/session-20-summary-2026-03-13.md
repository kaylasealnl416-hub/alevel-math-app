## 💡 Session 20 Content Additions

### 1. Globalisation (p3c1)

**Definition and Drivers**:
- Increasing interconnectedness and interdependence
- Drivers: Technology (internet, transport), economic (free trade), political (end of Cold War), ideological (neoliberalism)

**Economic Globalisation**:
- Trade: World trade grew 10x since 1950, free trade agreements, global value chains
- FDI: Foreign Direct Investment grew 20x since 1990
- TNCs: Transnational corporations (Apple, McDonald's, Nike)
- Financial markets: 24/7 trading, capital flows, 2008 financial crisis

**Political Globalisation**:
- International organisations: UN, WTO, IMF, World Bank, NATO, EU
- Global governance: Collective management without world government
- Sovereignty erosion: States can't control capital flows, IGOs constrain actions

**Cultural Globalisation**:
- Homogenisation: McDonaldisation, spread of Western culture
- Hybridisation: Mixing of cultures (Bollywood, K-pop, fusion cuisine)
- Identity: Cosmopolitanism vs nationalism

**Technological Drivers**:
- Internet: 5 billion users, instant communication, digital divide
- Transport: Air travel, containerisation, speed

**Winners and Losers**:
- Winners: Developed countries, emerging economies (China, India), TNCs, consumers, skilled workers
- Losers: Developed country workers (job losses), least developed countries, environment, local cultures, unskilled workers

**Inequality**:
- Between countries: Some converging (China, India), others diverging (Sub-Saharan Africa)
- Within countries: Top 1% gained most, middle class squeezed, working class lost jobs

**Anti-Globalisation Movements**:
- Protests: Seattle WTO (1999)
- Economic nationalism: Trump's trade war, Brexit
- Populism: Left (anti-corporate) and right (anti-immigration)

**Impact on Sovereignty**:
- Economic: Tax competition, regulatory competition, capital flight
- Political: IGOs constrain policies, humanitarian intervention

**Contemporary Issues**:
- Environment: Climate change, pollution, Paris Agreement
- Migration: 280 million international migrants
- Pandemics: COVID-19 spread, vaccine nationalism
- Terrorism: Global networks (Al-Qaeda, ISIS)

**Future Scenarios**:
- Hyperglobalisation: Accelerates, borders irrelevant
- Deglobalisation: Reverses, protectionism, Brexit, Trump tariffs
- Regionalisation: Regional blocs (EU, ASEAN)

### 2. International Organisations (p3c2)

**United Nations (UN)**:
- Founded 1945, 193 members
- Structure: General Assembly, Security Council (P5 with veto), Secretariat, ECOSOC, ICJ
- Security Council: 5 permanent (US, UK, France, Russia, China), 10 elected
- Peacekeeping: 87,000 personnel, successes (Cyprus, Namibia), failures (Rwanda, Srebrenica)
- Reform debates: Expand P5, abolish veto, funding issues
- Specialized agencies: WHO, UNESCO, UNICEF, UNHCR

**European Union (EU)**:
- History: ECSC (1951) → EEC (1957) → EU, 6 → 28 members (now 27 after Brexit)
- Institutions: Commission (executive), Council (ministers), Parliament (elected), European Council (heads), ECJ (court)
- Single market: Four freedoms (goods, services, capital, people)
- Powers: Supranational (EU law supreme), trade, competition, agriculture
- Brexit (2016-2020): 52% voted Leave, negotiations took 3.5 years, economic costs

**NATO**:
- Founded 1949, 32 members (2024)
- Article 5: Collective defense, invoked once (9/11)
- Expansion: Former Warsaw Pact countries, Baltic states, Finland (2023), Sweden (2024)
- Operations: Bosnia, Kosovo, Afghanistan (2001-2021), Libya
- Current challenges: Ukraine (Russia invaded 2022), defense spending (2% GDP), Trump questioned commitment

**World Trade Organization (WTO)**:
- Created 1995, 164 members
- Purpose: Promote free trade, reduce tariffs
- Dispute settlement: 600+ disputes resolved
- Crisis: US blocked Appellate Body (2019), Doha Round stalled

**International Monetary Fund (IMF)**:
- Created 1944 (Bretton Woods), 190 members
- Purpose: Monetary cooperation, financial stability
- Structural adjustment: Austerity conditions for loans
- Criticism: Hurts poor, serves rich countries, undemocratic

**World Bank**:
- Created 1944, purpose: Reduce poverty, promote development
- Criticism: Projects harm environment, serves rich countries
- Successes: Reduced extreme poverty

**Effectiveness**:
- Successes: UN prevented WWIII, EU peace, NATO deterred Soviet Union, WTO increased trade
- Failures: UN failed Rwanda/Syria, EU crises, NATO Afghanistan, WTO Doha stalled
- Factors: Member support, enforcement power, resources, legitimacy, sovereignty concerns

**Challenges**:
- Sovereignty: States reluctant to give up power
- Power imbalance: Dominated by US, EU, China
- Legitimacy: Democratic deficit, accountability
- Effectiveness: Bureaucracy, coordination, enforcement
- Geopolitical tensions: US-China rivalry, Russia, populism

### 3. International Relations Theories (p3c3)

**Realism**:
- Core assumptions: Anarchy, states main actors, seek power/security, self-help, zero-sum, conflict inevitable
- Classical realism: Human nature (Thucydides, Machiavelli, Hobbes, Morgenthau)
- Neorealism: Structure matters (Waltz), polarity (unipolar, bipolar, multipolar), balance of power
- Offensive vs defensive: Maximize power (Mearsheimer) vs seek security (Waltz)
- Key concepts: Balance of power, security dilemma, relative gains, hegemonic stability
- Strengths: Explains great power politics, Cold War, US-China rivalry
- Weaknesses: Ignores cooperation, domestic politics, ideas, pessimistic, amoral

**Liberalism**:
- Core assumptions: Cooperation possible, multiple actors, interdependence, institutions, positive-sum, progress
- Classical liberalism: Kant (perpetual peace), Wilson (League of Nations)
- Neoliberal institutionalism: Keohane and Nye, interdependence, institutions reduce costs
- Democratic peace theory: Democracies don't fight each other
- Commercial peace theory: Trade reduces war
- Key concepts: Complex interdependence, collective security, international law, soft power
- Strengths: Explains cooperation (EU, WTO), peace, optimistic
- Weaknesses: Ignores power, naive, Eurocentric, doesn't explain conflict

**Constructivism**:
- Core assumptions: Social construction, ideas matter, agents and structures mutually constituted, change possible
- Key thinkers: Wendt ('anarchy is what states make of it'), Finnemore, Katzenstein
- Identity: Who we are shapes what we want (US as 'leader of free world')
- Norms: Shared expectations (sovereignty, human rights, non-use of nuclear weapons)
- Culture: Strategic culture (US interventionist, Japan pacifist)
- Key concepts: Logic of appropriateness, socialization, constitutive effects
- Strengths: Explains change (norms), identity, ideas
- Weaknesses: Vague, ignores material factors, descriptive not predictive

**Comparison**:
- Realism vs Liberalism: Conflict vs cooperation, states vs multiple actors, pessimistic vs optimistic
- Realism vs Constructivism: Fixed interests vs socially constructed, material vs ideational
- Liberalism vs Constructivism: Functional institutions vs constitutive, rational vs logic of appropriateness

**Applications**:
- Cold War: Realism explains best (bipolar, balance of power), constructivism adds ideology
- European integration: Liberalism and constructivism explain better (interdependence, norms)
- War on Terror: All three offer insights (power, cooperation, identity)
- Climate change: Liberalism optimistic (institutions), realism pessimistic (free-rider)
- US-China rivalry: Realism predicts conflict (power transition), liberalism optimistic (interdependence)

**Debates**:
- Which theory is best? No single theory, use multiple
- Are theories relevant? Useful but imperfect
- Can theories coexist? Complement each other
