## 💡 Session 19 Content Additions

### 1. US Federalism & Constitution (p2c1)

**Constitutional Foundations (1787)**:
- Articles of Confederation (1781-1789) - weak central government
- Shays' Rebellion (1786-1787) - exposed weakness
- Constitutional Convention (May-September 1787)
- Founding Fathers: Washington, Madison, Hamilton, Franklin

**Key Compromises**:
- Great Compromise (Connecticut Compromise) - bicameral legislature
- Three-Fifths Compromise - slaves counted as 3/5
- Commerce Compromise - regulate commerce, no export taxes

**Constitutional Principles**:
- Popular sovereignty ('We the People')
- Limited government
- Separation of powers (Legislative, Executive, Judicial)
- Checks and balances
- Federalism (federal vs state power)
- Judicial review (Marbury v. Madison 1803)

**Separation of Powers**:
- Legislative (Congress): Makes laws
- Executive (President): Enforces laws
- Judicial (Supreme Court): Interprets laws
- Rationale: Prevent tyranny, 'ambition must counteract ambition'

**Checks and Balances**:
- Congress checks: Override veto (2/3), approve appointments, ratify treaties, impeachment, control budget
- President checks: Veto, appoint judges, pardon
- Courts check: Judicial review - declare laws unconstitutional

**Division of Powers**:
- Enumerated powers (federal): Coin money, regulate commerce, declare war, treaties
- Reserved powers (states): Elections, schools, marriage, criminal law (10th Amendment)
- Concurrent powers (shared): Tax, borrow, establish courts, make laws
- Supremacy Clause: Federal law overrides state law

**Evolution of Federalism**:
- Dual federalism (1789-1930s): 'Layer cake' - separate spheres
- Cooperative federalism (1930s-1960s): 'Marble cake' - work together, New Deal
- New federalism (1970s-1990s): Return power to states, block grants
- Contemporary (2000s-present): Partisan, selective federalism

**Supreme Court Cases**:
- Expanding federal power: McCulloch v. Maryland (1819), Gibbons v. Ogden (1824), Wickard v. Filburn (1942)
- Limiting federal power: US v. Lopez (1995), Printz v. US (1997), NFIB v. Sebelius (2012)

**Contemporary Issues**:
- Healthcare: Affordable Care Act, state resistance
- Immigration: Arizona SB 1070, sanctuary cities
- Marijuana: Federal illegal, 24 states legalized recreational
- Gun control: Federal vs state laws
- Same-sex marriage: Obergefell v. Hodges (2015)
- Abortion: Dobbs v. Jackson (2022) - returned to states

**Comparison with UK**:
- US: Federal (power divided), written constitution, divided sovereignty
- UK: Unitary (power centralized), unwritten constitution, parliamentary sovereignty
- UK devolution: Scotland, Wales, Northern Ireland (1998-1999)

### 2. US Congress (p2c2)

**Bicameral Structure**:
- House of Representatives: 435 members, 2-year terms, by population
- Senate: 100 members (2 per state), 6-year terms, equal representation
- Great Compromise (1787): House by population, Senate equal

**House Characteristics**:
- Larger (435 vs 100), more formal rules, less prestigious
- More responsive (2-year terms)
- Initiates revenue bills
- Speaker of the House (most powerful)

**Senate Characteristics**:
- Smaller (100), less formal rules, more prestigious
- More deliberative ('cooling saucer')
- Exclusive powers: Ratify treaties (2/3), confirm appointments, try impeachments
- Filibuster: Unlimited debate, 60 votes for cloture

**Powers**:
- Tax and spend, regulate commerce, declare war
- House exclusive: Initiate revenue bills, impeachment charges
- Senate exclusive: Ratify treaties, confirm appointments, try impeachments

**Legislative Process**:
1. Bill introduced
2. Committee stage (most bills die here)
3. Floor debate (House: Rules Committee, Senate: unlimited debate)
4. Conference committee (reconcile differences)
5. Presidential action (sign, veto, pocket veto)
- Only 2-3% of bills become law

**Committee System**:
- Standing committees (permanent, subject-specific)
- Committee chairs (significant power, traditionally by seniority)
- Key committees: Ways and Means, Appropriations, Judiciary, Armed Services, Rules (House)

**Party Leadership**:
- House: Speaker (most powerful), Majority/Minority Leaders, Whips
- Senate: VP (President of Senate), Majority/Minority Leaders, Whips
- Recent leaders: Pelosi, Boehner, Ryan, McCarthy, Johnson (House); McConnell, Schumer (Senate)

**Filibuster**:
- Unlimited debate in Senate
- Cloture: 60 votes to end debate
- Used to block civil rights (1950s-1960s)
- Modern: Requires 60 votes for most legislation
- Exceptions: Budget reconciliation (simple majority)

**Checks and Balances**:
- On President: Override veto, approve appointments, ratify treaties, impeachment, power of purse
- On Courts: Confirm judges, impeachment, create courts, constitutional amendments

**Representation Theories**:
- Trustee: Use own judgment (Burke)
- Delegate: Follow constituents' wishes
- Politico: Combination (most common)

**Elections**:
- House: Every 2 years, 90%+ incumbency advantage
- Senate: Every 6 years (staggered), 80-85% incumbency advantage
- Gerrymandering: Drawing districts to favor one party (packing, cracking)

**Contemporary Issues**:
- Polarization: Increasing ideological distance, fewer moderates, party-line voting
- Gridlock: Divided government, difficulty passing legislation
- Government shutdowns: 2013 (16 days), 2018-2019 (35 days - longest)
- Impeachment: Increasingly partisan (Clinton, Trump twice)

**Comparison with UK Parliament**:
- Both bicameral, but US Senate more powerful
- US: Fixed terms, separate executive, weaker party discipline, powerful committees
- UK: Flexible terms, fused executive, stronger party discipline, weaker committees

### 3. US Presidency (p2c3)

**Constitutional Basis**:
- Article II: Executive power vested in President
- Vague (shorter than Article I)
- Single executive (not committee)
- Electoral College (indirect election)
- 4-year term, 22nd Amendment (1951) limits to 2 terms

**Qualifications**:
- Natural-born citizen, at least 35 years old, 14 years residency
- Informal: Traditionally white male, wealthy, political experience

**Presidential Roles**:
- Chief Executive: Enforce laws, manage executive branch
- Commander-in-Chief: Lead military
- Chief Diplomat: Conduct foreign policy
- Chief Legislator: Propose legislation, veto bills
- Chief of State: Ceremonial head
- Party Leader, Economic Manager

**Formal Powers**:
- Executive: Execute laws, appoint officials, executive orders, pardon
- Military: Commander-in-Chief, deploy troops, War Powers Resolution (1973)
- Foreign policy: Make treaties, executive agreements, recognize nations
- Legislative: Veto, State of Union, recommend legislation

**Informal Powers**:
- Executive orders: Increasingly used to bypass Congress
- Executive agreements: Used more than treaties (don't need Senate)
- Signing statements: President's interpretation of law
- Bully pulpit: Use media to shape public opinion (Theodore Roosevelt)
- Crisis leadership: Power grows during crises

**Executive Orders**:
- Presidential directives to executive branch
- Have force of law, but can be overturned
- Examples: Emancipation Proclamation, Japanese internment, DACA, travel ban
- Modern Presidents issue 30-50 per year

**Executive Agreements**:
- Agreements with foreign nations without Senate approval
- 90% of international agreements are executive agreements
- Examples: Paris Climate Agreement, Iran nuclear deal

**Cabinet**:
- 15 executive departments (State, Treasury, Defense, Justice, etc.)
- President appoints, Senate confirms
- Role: Advise President, manage departments

**Executive Office of the President (EOP)**:
- Created 1939, about 1,800 employees
- White House Office: Chief of Staff, Press Secretary, advisers
- OMB: Prepares budget
- NSC: National security advice
- EOP staff often more influential than Cabinet

**Presidential Elections**:
- Primaries: Parties choose nominees (Iowa, New Hampshire first)
- General election: Electoral College (538 electors, 270 to win)
- Winner-take-all: Most states give all electors to plurality winner
- Swing states: Florida, Pennsylvania, Michigan, Wisconsin, Arizona, Georgia

**Electoral College**:
- 538 electors (435 House + 100 Senate + 3 DC)
- 5 Presidents won Electoral College but lost popular vote (1824, 1876, 1888, 2000, 2016)
- Debate: Protects small states vs undemocratic
- Reform: National Popular Vote Interstate Compact (needs 270 electors, currently 209)

**Relationship with Congress**:
- Cooperation: Unified government, honeymoon period, party loyalty
- Conflict: Divided government, veto, investigations, impeachment, power of purse

**Limits on Power**:
- Congress: Override veto, refuse legislation/appointments, impeachment
- Courts: Declare actions unconstitutional
- Bureaucracy: Career civil servants resist
- Public opinion: Low approval limits influence
- Media: Investigates and criticizes
- Federalism: States resist
- Term limits: 22nd Amendment

**Imperial vs Imperiled Presidency**:
- Imperial: Too much power (war powers, executive orders, surveillance)
- Imperiled: Too little power (gridlock, can't pass legislation, investigations)
- Debate: Has power grown or declined? Varies by issue, circumstance, President

**Presidential Approval**:
- Honeymoon: Usually high at start
- Decline: Usually declines over time
- Rally effect: Rises during crises (9/11, Gulf War)
- Polarization: Increasingly partisan
- Importance: High approval = more influence

**Recent Presidents**:
- Obama (2009-2017): First Black President, Affordable Care Act, faced Republican Congress
- Trump (2017-2021): Businessman, Tax Cuts, 3 Supreme Court justices, impeached twice, January 6
- Biden (2021-present): Oldest President, infrastructure bill, Afghanistan withdrawal, Ukraine support

**Comparison with UK Prime Minister**:
- Selection: US - Electoral College, UK - leader of majority party
- Term: US - fixed 4 years, UK - flexible (up to 5 years)
- Separation: US - separate from Congress, UK - member of Parliament
- Powers: US - veto, commander-in-chief, appoint judges; UK - no veto, less military power
- Checks: US - strong (Congress, courts), UK - weak (government usually has majority)
