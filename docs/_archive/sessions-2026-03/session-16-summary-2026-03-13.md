### 2. Market Equilibrium (e1c4)

**Equilibrium Adjustment Process**:
- Surplus adjustment (price above equilibrium → price falls)
- Shortage adjustment (price below equilibrium → price rises)
- Speed of adjustment (financial markets vs housing markets)

**Price Mechanism Functions Expanded**:
- Signalling (high prices signal scarcity)
- Incentivising (high prices encourage production)
- Rationing (allocates to those willing to pay)
- Allocating (resources flow to highest-value uses)

**Joint Demand and Supply Shifts**:
- 4 scenarios with definite and ambiguous effects
- Graphical analysis of simultaneous shifts

**Real-World Examples**:
- UK housing market (300% price rise 2000-2020)
- Oil market 2020 COVID crash (negative prices!)
- 2020 face mask shortage (500% price increase)
- Agricultural markets and the 'farm problem'

**Price Controls**:
- Price ceiling (maximum price): Creates shortages, black markets, quality deterioration
- Price ceiling example: New York rent controls, Venezuela price controls

- Price floor examples: EU butter mountain, minimum wage debate

**Consumer and Producer Surplus at Equilibrium**:
- Total welfare maximization at equilibrium
- Deadweight loss from interventions

**Market Efficiency**:
- Allocative efficiency (P = MC)
- Pareto efficiency

**Market Disequilibrium**:
- Causes (price controls, time lags, shocks, market power)
- Consequences (shortages/surpluses, black markets, inefficiency)

### 3. Economic Performance Measures (e2c1)

**GDP Concepts Expanded**:
- GDP vs GNI distinction
- Nominal vs Real GDP (detailed explanation)
- GDP per capita (better living standards measure)
- Three calculation methods (expenditure, income, output)

**GDP Limitations**:
- Excludes non-market activities (housework, volunteering)
- Excludes informal economy (black market, up to 40% in developing countries)
- Ignores distribution (inequality not captured)
- Ignores quality of life (happiness, health, environment)
- Includes 'bads' (pollution cleanup increases GDP)
- Doesn't account for sustainability (resource depletion)

**Alternative Measures**:
- HDI (Human Development Index): GDP + life expectancy + education
- GPI (Genuine Progress Indicator): Adjusts for distribution and environment
- Happiness Index: Bhutan's Gross National Happiness

**Inflation Expanded**:
- CPI vs RPI vs GDP Deflator (differences and uses)
- Demand-pull inflation (excess AD)
- Cost-push inflation (rising costs)
- Real-world examples: 1970s oil crisis, Zimbabwe hyperinflation
- Effects on consumers, firms, government
- Deflation dangers (Japan 1990s-2000s)

**Unemployment Types Expanded**:
- Frictional (between jobs, 2-3%)
- Structural (skills mismatch, UK coal mining example)
- Seasonal (tourism, agriculture, retail)
- Cyclical (demand-deficient, most serious)
- Costs to individuals, government, economy

**Balance of Payments Expanded**:
- Current account components (goods, services, income, transfers)
- Trade in goods (UK deficit ~£100bn)
- Trade in services (UK surplus ~£100bn, strong finance)
- Current account deficit/surplus causes and consequences

### 4. Aggregate Supply (e2c3)

**SRAS Detailed Analysis**:
- Why upward sloping (fixed wages, money illusion)
- Rightward shifts (lower costs, productivity, subsidies)
- Leftward shifts (higher costs, disasters, taxes)
- Real examples: 1973 oil crisis, 2021-2022 supply chain crisis

**Classical vs Keynesian LRAS**:
- Classical: Vertical at full employment, markets clear, self-correcting
- Keynesian: Three sections (horizontal-upward-vertical)
- Section 1: Deep recession, spare capacity, AD increase → output rises, prices stable
- Section 2: Approaching capacity, bottlenecks, AD increase → output and prices rise
- Section 3: Full capacity, AD increase → only prices rise
- Policy implications of each view

**Output Gap Analysis**:
- Negative gap (recessionary): Actual < Potential, high unemployment, deflation risk
- Negative gap example: 2008-2009 UK output gap -4%
- Positive gap (inflationary): Actual > Potential, overheating, inflation pressure
- Positive gap example: 2021-2022 post-COVID boom
- Measurement difficulty (potential GDP unobservable)

**LRAS Shifts**:
- Rightward shift factors: Technology, capital accumulation, labor force growth, education, natural resources
- Institutional factors: Property rights, rule of law, competition, trade openness
- Real examples: China 1980-2020 (10% annual growth), UK Industrial Revolution
- Leftward shift (rare): Natural disasters, war, brain drain

**Supply Shocks**:
- Positive shocks: Technology breakthrough, oil price collapse, good harvest
- Negative shocks: Oil crisis, natural disaster, pandemic, war
- Real examples: COVID-19 pandemic (2020), fracking revolution (2010s)

**Natural Rate of Unemployment**:
- Definition (frictional + structural, excludes cyclical)
- Full employment doesn't mean zero unemployment
- UK natural rate ~4-5%

**Long-Run Equilibrium**:
- AD intersects LRAS
- Actual GDP = Potential GDP
- Unemployment at natural rate
- No inflationary/deflationary pressure

**Examples Added**: 6 examples (from 1 to 6)
- SRAS vs LRAS explanation
- Output gap calculation with policy recommendations
- Classical vs Keynesian comparison
- Russia-Ukraine war AS-AD analysis (stagflation)
- Cyclical unemployment calculation
- Technology progress and LRAS shift (internet revolution)

**Formulas Added**: 5 formulas (from 2 to 5)
- Output gap percentage formula
- Growth gap formula
- Natural rate of unemployment formula
- Cyclical unemployment formula
