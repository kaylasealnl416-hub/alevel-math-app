### Major Additions

#### 1. Detailed BOP Account Structure
- ✅ **Current Account Components**: Trade in goods (visible), Trade in services (invisible), Primary income (investment income), Secondary income (transfers) - all explained with examples
- ✅ **Capital Account**: Capital transfers, non-produced assets (patents, copyrights)
- ✅ **Financial Account**: FDI (long-term), Portfolio investment (short-term), Other investment, Reserve assets
- ✅ **BOP Identity**: Current + Capital + Financial = 0 (double-entry bookkeeping explained)

#### 2. Current Account Analysis
- ✅ **Deficit Analysis**: Causes (strong currency, high imports, low competitiveness), Is it a problem? (depends on size, sustainability, cause, financing method), Risks (debt accumulation, depreciation pressure, capital flight)
- ✅ **Surplus Analysis**: Causes (weak currency, high savings, strong competitiveness), Is it a problem? (insufficient demand, currency manipulation, global imbalances)

#### 3. Graphical Analysis
- ✅ **BOP Structure Diagram**: Hierarchical chart showing all components and how they balance

#### 4. Adjustment Mechanisms
- ✅ **Expenditure-Reducing Policies**: Fiscal/monetary contraction to reduce imports (problem: recession)
- ✅ **Expenditure-Switching Policies**: Depreciation/devaluation to switch spending (problem: J-curve, inflation)
- ✅ **Supply-Side Policies**: Improve competitiveness (advantage: no side effects, problem: takes years)

#### 5. Real-World Case Studies (5 examples)
- ✅ **US Current Account Deficit (1980-present)**: $800bn deficit (3% GDP), financed by foreigners buying US assets, sustainable due to reserve currency status
- ✅ **China Current Account Surplus (2000-present)**: Peak $420bn (10% GDP), undervalued yuan, high savings, export-oriented model, accumulated $3 trillion reserves
- ✅ **Germany Current Account Surplus (2000-present)**: €250bn (7% GDP, largest in world), strong manufacturing, criticized by IMF for insufficient domestic demand
- ✅ **UK Current Account Deficit (1980-present)**: £80bn (3% GDP), deindustrialization, strong pound, offset by services surplus (financial services)
- ✅ **Asian Financial Crisis (1997-1998)**: Thailand, Indonesia, South Korea deficits (5-8% GDP) financed by hot money → capital flight → currencies collapsed 50-80% → IMF bailouts

## 🎯 Inequality & Poverty (e4c5) - Comprehensive Enhancement

### Content Growth
- **Before**: 8 key points
- **After**: 36 key points
- **Growth**: +350% (28 new points)

### Major Additions

#### 1. Income vs Wealth Distinction
- ✅ **Income**: Flow of money (wages, profits, rent, interest) - measured per year
- ✅ **Wealth**: Stock of assets (property, savings, shares, pensions) - measured at point in time
- ✅ Wealth inequality typically much greater than income inequality

#### 2. Detailed Graphical Analysis
- ✅ **Lorenz Curve**: Graph showing cumulative % of income vs cumulative % of population, line of perfect equality (45-degree diagonal), actual curve bows below
- ✅ **Lorenz Curve Interpretation**: If bottom 40% earn 15% → top 60% earn 85%. Curve closer to diagonal = more equal
- ✅ **Gini Coefficient**: Numerical measure (0 = perfect equality, 1 = perfect inequality). Formula: Area between curves / Total area. Typical values: 0.25-0.35 (equal), 0.35-0.45 (moderate), 0.45-0.65 (unequal)
- ✅ **Gini Interpretation**: Gini = 0.30 means if you pick two people randomly, income difference = 30% of average

#### 3. Comprehensive Causes of Inequality (7 causes)
- ✅ **Education and Skills**: University graduates earn 50% more, skill premium increased due to technology
- ✅ **Wage Differentials**: Marginal productivity, scarcity, barriers to entry, discrimination, unions, monopsony
- ✅ **Wealth Ownership**: Wealth generates income (rent, dividends, interest) - £1 million at 5% = £50,000 passive income
- ✅ **Inheritance**: Intergenerational wealth transfer - UK average £100,000, top 10% inherit £500,000+
- ✅ **Technology**: Automation replaces low-skill jobs, increases demand for high-skill workers (skill-biased technological change)
- ✅ **Globalisation**: Offshoring reduces demand for low-skill workers in developed countries
- ✅ **Discrimination**: Gender pay gap (15%), ethnic pay gap (10%)
- ✅ **Market Power**: CEO pay increased 1000% since 1980, average worker 12% - FTSE 100 CEO = £3.5 million (120x median)

#### 4. Poverty Analysis
- ✅ **Absolute Poverty**: Below minimum for basic necessities - World Bank $2.15/day, 700 million people (9% of world)
- ✅ **Relative Poverty**: Below 60% of median income - UK 14 million (22%)
- ✅ **Poverty Traps**: Unemployment trap (benefits withdrawn when working), low skills trap, debt trap, health trap
- ✅ **Consequences**: Economic (lower growth, inefficient allocation, financial instability), Social (crime, health, social mobility, political instability)

#### 5. Redistribution Policies (6 policies)
- ✅ **Progressive Taxation**: UK income tax 0%/20%/40%/45% - reduces post-tax inequality
- ✅ **Transfer Payments**: UK welfare £250bn (30% of spending) - unemployment, child, housing benefits
- ✅ **National Minimum Wage**: UK £10.42 - raises low-paid incomes, minimal unemployment effect
- ✅ **Universal Basic Services**: Free education, healthcare (NHS £150bn/year)
- ✅ **Wealth Taxes**: Inheritance tax (40% over £325,000), capital gains tax (20%)
- ✅ **Education and Training**: Investment in human capital - addresses root cause

#### 6. Real-World Case Studies (4 examples)
- ✅ **US Income Inequality (1980-present)**: Gini rose 0.34 → 0.41 (highest in developed world). Causes: Technology, globalization, declining unions (25% → 10%), tax cuts (top rate 70% → 37%), CEO pay explosion (350x median). Top 1% share rose 10% → 20%
- ✅ **Nordic Model (Sweden, Denmark, Norway)**: Low inequality (Gini 0.25-0.28) despite high GDP. Policies: High progressive taxes (60%), generous welfare (80% unemployment benefits), free education/healthcare, strong unions (70%), active labor market policies. Result: Low poverty (5%), high social mobility
- ✅ **UK Inequality Trends (1980-present)**: Gini rose 0.25 → 0.35 (1979-1990) then stabilized. Causes: Thatcher policies, deindustrialization, financial sector growth. Policies: Minimum wage (1999), tax credits, Sure Start. Relative poverty fell 25% → 22%
- ✅ **China Inequality (1980-present)**: Gini rose 0.30 → 0.47. Causes: Urban-rural divide (3x wages), coastal-inland divide, hukou system, SOE privatization. 800 million lifted from absolute poverty but inequality increased. Government response: Abolishing agricultural tax, rural development, minimum wage, crackdown on billionaires

## 🎯 Economic Development (e4c6) - Comprehensive Enhancement

### Content Growth
- **Before**: 11 key points
- **After**: 43 key points
- **Growth**: +291% (32 new points)

### Major Additions

#### 1. Growth vs Development Distinction
- ✅ **Economic Growth**: Increase in real GDP (quantity) - China 10% per year (1980-2010)
- ✅ **Economic Development**: Broader concept (living standards, health, education, freedom, equality, sustainability) - growth necessary but not sufficient

#### 2. Detailed HDI Analysis
- ✅ **HDI Components**: Life expectancy, Education (mean + expected years), GNI per capita PPP
- ✅ **HDI Categories**: Very high (>0.8): Norway, Switzerland, Australia. High (0.7-0.8): China, Brazil. Medium (0.55-0.7): India, Indonesia. Low (<0.55): Sub-Saharan Africa
- ✅ **HDI Advantages**: Broader than GDP, easy to understand, allows comparisons, highlights priorities
- ✅ **HDI Disadvantages**: Ignores inequality, ignores freedom/rights/environment, data quality issues, equal weighting arbitrary

#### 3. Other Development Indicators
- ✅ **Health**: Life expectancy (UK 81, Niger 62), Infant mortality (UK 4, Niger 50 per 1000), Maternal mortality, Clean water access
- ✅ **Education**: Literacy rate (UK 99%, Niger 30%), Mean years of schooling (UK 13, Niger 2), Primary enrollment, Gender parity
- ✅ **Inequality**: Gini coefficient, Income share of bottom 40%, Multidimensional Poverty Index
- ✅ **Environment**: CO2 emissions per capita (US 15 tons, India 2 tons), Forest coverage, Electricity access, Renewable energy

#### 4. Emerging Economies
- ✅ **BRICS**: Brazil, Russia, India, China, South Africa - fast growth (5-10%), rising middle class, urbanization, industrialization
- ✅ **China**: 10% growth (1980-2010), 800 million out of poverty, 2nd largest economy ($18 trillion) - export-led growth, FDI, infrastructure
- ✅ **India**: 7% growth (2000-2020), 1.4 billion population, IT services hub, but 20% in poverty - services-led growth, democracy

#### 5. Barriers to Development (9 barriers)
- ✅ **Low Capital Accumulation**: Low savings → low investment → low growth (Harrod-Domar model)
- ✅ **Poor Human Capital**: Low education/health → low productivity → poverty trap
- ✅ **Corruption**: Nigeria lost $400bn (1960-2000), equivalent to all Western aid to Africa
- ✅ **Debt Burden**: Zambia debt service = 30% of revenue → only 10% for health
- ✅ **Capital Flight**: Africa loses $50bn/year (more than receives in aid)
- ✅ **Poor Infrastructure**: Sub-Saharan Africa electricity 50% → factories can't operate
- ✅ **Political Instability**: Syria civil war → GDP fell 60%, 6 million refugees
- ✅ **Geography**: 16 of 20 poorest countries landlocked or tropical
- ✅ **Primary Product Dependency**: Zambia copper 70% of exports → price fell 50% → crisis

#### 6. Development Strategies (4 strategies)
- ✅ **Import Substitution (ISI)**: Protect domestic industries with tariffs - used by Latin America, India (1950s-90s). Advantage: Protects infant industries. Disadvantage: Inefficiency, high prices. Example: India ISI → 3% growth → liberalized (1991) → 7% growth
- ✅ **Export-Led Growth**: Focus on exports - used by East Asian Tigers, China. Advantage: Economies of scale, competition, foreign currency. Disadvantage: Vulnerable to recessions. Example: South Korea (1960-1990) → GDP per capita $100 → $10,000
- ✅ **Agricultural Development**: Green Revolution, land reform, extension services. Example: India Green Revolution (1960s-70s) → wheat production doubled → famine eliminated
- ✅ **Microfinance**: Small loans to poor (especially women) - Grameen Bank (Bangladesh, Nobel Prize 2006). Advantage: Empowers poor, 98% repayment. Disadvantage: High interest (20-30%), debt traps

#### 7. Foreign Aid Analysis
- ✅ **Types**: Bilateral, multilateral, humanitarian, development - total $200bn/year (0.3% of rich countries' GDP, target 0.7%)
- ✅ **Advantages**: Fills savings gap, fills foreign exchange gap, technical expertise, humanitarian relief. Example: Marshall Plan ($13bn) → Europe recovered
- ✅ **Disadvantages**: Dependency culture, corruption, tied aid, undermines local industries, debt burden. Example: Zambia received $18bn (1960-2000) but GDP per capita fell

#### 8. FDI Analysis
- ✅ **Advantages**: Capital inflow, technology transfer, jobs, tax revenue, multiplier effect, exports. Example: China attracted $1.5 trillion FDI (1980-2020) → key growth driver
- ✅ **Disadvantages**: Profit repatriation, exploitation (low wages), environmental damage, crowding out, dependency. Example: Nike Vietnam → $2/day wages, poor conditions, but 500,000 jobs

#### 9. Sustainable Development
- ✅ **Definition**: Meet present needs without compromising future generations (Brundtland 1987) - UN SDGs: 17 goals (end poverty, quality education, clean energy, climate action)
- ✅ **Environmental Sustainability**: Reduce CO2, protect forests, reduce pollution. Trade-off: Growth vs environment. Example: China prioritized growth → severe pollution → now investing in renewables
- ✅ **Social Sustainability**: Reduce inequality, improve health/education, gender equality. Example: Costa Rica → high HDI (0.81) despite modest GDP ($12,000) through education, healthcare, environment (98% renewable)

#### 10. Real-World Case Studies (4 examples)
- ✅ **South Korea Development Miracle (1960-1990)**: GDP per capita $100 → $10,000 → $35,000 (2023). Strategy: Export-led growth (electronics, cars), government support for chaebols (Samsung, Hyundai), education investment (literacy 100%, university 70%), land reform, authoritarian government. Lesson: Export-led + education + government support works
- ✅ **Botswana Development Success (1966-2020)**: GDP per capita $400 → $8,000 (highest in Africa). Strategy: Diamond exports (40% GDP), good governance (low corruption, democracy), education/health investment (free primary, HIV treatment), prudent fiscal policy (saved revenues). Fastest-growing economy (1966-2000). Lesson: Natural resources + good governance = development
- ✅ **Bangladesh Garment Industry (1980-present)**: Garment exports $0 → $40bn (80% of exports), 4 million workers (mostly women). Strategy: Low wages ($100/month), Export Processing Zones, microfinance. Impact: Poverty 80% → 20%, life expectancy 50 → 73, literacy 30% → 75%. Criticism: Poor conditions (Rana Plaza 1,100 deaths), low wages. Lesson: Export-led reduces poverty but raises labor/environmental concerns
- ✅ **Venezuela Economic Collapse (2013-present)**: GDP fell 75%, inflation 1,000,000% (2018), 7 million refugees. Causes: Oil dependency (95% exports) → price fell $100 → $30, corruption (Chavez/Maduro), price controls → shortages, printing money → hyperinflation, sanctions. Result: Poverty 90%, hunger, healthcare collapse. Lesson: Resource curse - oil wealth can harm development if poorly managed
