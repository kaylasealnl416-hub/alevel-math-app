## 💡 Session 21 Content Additions

### Comparative Politics Methods (p4c1)

**Definition and Scope**:
- Study of comparing political systems across countries
- Questions: Why different systems? Why some democracies succeed?
- Comparative method: Most Similar Systems Design (MSSD), Most Different Systems Design (MDSD)
- Challenges: Too many variables, too few cases, selection bias, measurement, context

**Political Systems Comparison**:
- Presidential: President separate from legislature (US, Brazil, Mexico)
  - Advantages: Stability, checks and balances, clear accountability
  - Disadvantages: Gridlock, winner-take-all, difficult to remove
- Parliamentary: PM member of legislature (UK, Germany, Japan, India)
  - Advantages: Efficiency, flexibility, coalition governments
  - Disadvantages: Less checks, less stability, less clear accountability
- Semi-presidential: President and PM share power (France, Russia)
  - Cohabitation: President and PM from different parties

**Federal vs Unitary Systems**:
- Federal: Power divided (US, Germany, India, Brazil)
  - Advantages: Diversity, experimentation, prevents tyranny
  - Disadvantages: Complexity, inefficiency, inequality
- Unitary: Power centralized (UK, France, Japan, China)
  - Advantages: Efficiency, uniformity, clarity
  - Disadvantages: Less diversity, more centralized power

**Regime Types**:
- Democracy: Free elections, civil liberties, rule of law
  - Liberal democracy vs electoral democracy
  - Measures: Freedom House, Polity IV
- Authoritarianism: Limited competition, restricted liberties
  - Types: Military, one-party, personalist
  - Examples: China, Russia, Saudi Arabia
- Totalitarianism: Total control, ideology, terror
  - Examples: Nazi Germany, Stalinist USSR, North Korea

**Democratization**:
- Three waves: 1828-1926, 1943-1962, 1974-present
- Reverse waves: 1922-1942 (fascism), 1958-1975 (coups), 2000s-present (backsliding)
- Preconditions: Economic development, middle class, education, civil society, political culture
- Transition theories: Structural (development), agency (leaders' choices), stages (liberalization, transition, consolidation)
- Consolidation: Two turnover test, no anti-democratic actors
- Backsliding: Hungary, Turkey, Venezuela

**Political Culture and Socialization**:
- Political culture: Shared beliefs, values, attitudes
- Civic culture: Trust, participation, tolerance (Almond and Verba)
- Types: Parochial, subject, participant
- Socialization: Family, school, peers, media

**Case Studies**:
- United States: Presidential, federal, gridlock, polarization
- United Kingdom: Parliamentary, unitary (with devolution), Brexit
- Germany: Parliamentary, federal, learned from Weimar, coalition governments
- France: Semi-presidential, cohabitation, Yellow Vest protests
- China: One-party authoritarian, economic success, political control, Xi Jinping
- Russia: Semi-presidential, Putin, authoritarian consolidation, Ukraine invasion
- India: Parliamentary, federal, world's largest democracy, Modi and BJP
- Brazil: Presidential, federal, corruption, Bolsonaro and Lula
- South Africa: Parliamentary, apartheid to democracy, ANC dominated, inequality

**Comparative Analysis**:
- Presidential vs Parliamentary: Stability, efficiency, representation, accountability
- Democracy vs Authoritarianism: Economic growth, stability, legitimacy, human rights
- Developed vs Developing Democracies: Institutions, corruption, inequality, ethnic conflict

**Current Trends**:
- Democratic backsliding: Hungary, Turkey, Venezuela, Poland
- Populism: Left (Latin America) and right (Europe, US)
- Authoritarianism: China model, digital authoritarianism
- Polarization: US, UK, many democracies
- COVID-19: Increased executive power, surveillance
