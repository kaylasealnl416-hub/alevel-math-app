### Content Growth

### Major Additions

#### 1. Causes - Detailed Analysis (3 sections)
- ✅ **Financial Crisis**: Seven Years' War cost 1.3 billion livres, American Revolution support, tax system unfair (First/Second Estates exempt, Third Estate paid all), Louis XVI called Estates-General (May 1789), harvest failures (1788) doubled bread prices
- ✅ **Enlightenment Ideas**: Voltaire (freedom of speech), Rousseau (social contract), Montesquieu (separation of powers), American Revolution (1776) inspired French
- ✅ **Social Inequality (Ancien Régime)**: First Estate (clergy 0.5%, owned 10% land, tax-exempt), Second Estate (nobility 1.5%, owned 25% land, tax-exempt), Third Estate (98%, owned 65% land, paid all taxes)

#### 2. Revolutionary Stages (3 phases)
- ✅ **Stage 1 - Moderate Phase (1789-1792)**: Storming of Bastille (July 14, 1789), Declaration of Rights of Man (August 26, 1789), Women's March on Versailles (October 1789), Constitution (1791) established constitutional monarchy
- ✅ **Stage 2 - Radical Phase (1792-1794)**: Monarchy overthrown (August 10, 1792), Republic declared (September 1792), Louis XVI executed (January 21, 1793), Jacobins seized power
- ✅ **Stage 3 - Directory Period (1795-1799)**: Directory (5-man executive), economic crisis, Napoleon's coup (November 9, 1799)

#### 3. Reign of Terror (1793-1794)
- ✅ Led by Robespierre and Committee of Public Safety
- ✅ 16,000-40,000 executed (including Marie Antoinette)
- ✅ Law of Suspects, de-Christianization campaign
- ✅ Ended July 27, 1794 - Robespierre executed

#### 4. Napoleon's Rise and Fall (1799-1815)
- ✅ **Rise (1799-1804)**: First Consul (1799), Napoleonic Code (1804), Concordat (1801), crowned Emperor (1804)
- ✅ **Empire (1804-1815)**: Controlled most of Europe by 1810, Continental System (1806), Peninsular War (1808-1814), Russian Campaign (1812) - 600,000 troops, only 100,000 survived
- ✅ **Fall (1813-1815)**: Battle of Leipzig (October 1813), abdicated (April 1814), Hundred Days (March 1815), Battle of Waterloo (June 18, 1815), exiled to St. Helena

#### 5. Long-Term Impact (3 sections)
- ✅ **France**: End of absolute monarchy, end of feudalism, secularization, Napoleonic Code, nationalism, political instability (5 republics, 2 empires, 3 monarchies in 19th century)
- ✅ **Europe**: Spread of nationalism, liberal ideas, conservative reaction (Congress of Vienna 1815), Revolutions of 1830 and 1848, end of feudalism
- ✅ **World**: Inspired Latin American independence (1810s-1820s), Haitian Revolution (1791-1804), human rights (UN Declaration 1948), left-right political spectrum, total war (mass conscription)

#### 6. Key Figures (3 people)
- ✅ **Louis XVI (1754-1793)**: Weak leader, failed to solve financial crisis, executed January 21, 1793 - first European monarch executed by his own people
- ✅ **Robespierre (1758-1794)**: Jacobin leader, 'The Incorruptible', led Reign of Terror, executed July 28, 1794 - victim of his own Terror
- ✅ **Napoleon (1769-1821)**: Military genius, spread revolutionary ideas across Europe, ruled as dictator/emperor, legacy: Napoleonic Code, modern bureaucracy, nationalism

#### 7. Historiographical Debates (4 debates)

## 🎯 World War I (h1c2) - Comprehensive Enhancement

### Content Growth
- **Before**: 5 key points
- **After**: 28 key points
- **Growth**: +460% (23 new points)

### Major Additions

#### 1. Long-Term Causes - MAIN (4 sections)
- ✅ **Militarism**: Arms race, Germany doubled army (1890-1914), Britain-Germany naval race (Dreadnoughts), military spending +50% (1900-1914), Schlieffen Plan, glorification of war
- ✅ **Alliance System**: Triple Alliance (Germany, Austria-Hungary, Italy 1882), Triple Entente (Britain, France, Russia 1907), blank check to Austria-Hungary, chain reaction turned local conflict into world war
- ✅ **Imperialism**: Scramble for Africa (1880s-1914), Moroccan Crises (1905, 1911), Balkan Wars (1912-1913), economic rivalry (Germany's steel production surpassed Britain by 1900), naval rivalry
- ✅ **Nationalism**: Pan-Slavism (Russia supported Slavs), Serbian nationalism (Greater Serbia), French revanchism (recover Alsace-Lorraine), German nationalism ('place in the sun'), decline of Ottoman and Austrian empires ('powder keg of Europe')

#### 2. Immediate Trigger and July Crisis
- ✅ **Assassination**: June 28, 1914 - Archduke Franz Ferdinand assassinated in Sarajevo by Gavrilo Princip (Black Hand), Austrian ultimatum (July 23), Serbia rejected one demand, Austria-Hungary declared war (July 28)
- ✅ **Chain Reaction**: Russia mobilized (July 30), Germany declared war on Russia (August 1) and France (August 3), invaded Belgium, Britain declared war on Germany (August 4) - within one week, all major powers at war

#### 3. War Phases (3 phases)
- ✅ **Phase 1 - War of Movement (August-November 1914)**: Schlieffen Plan failed, Battle of the Marne (September 6-12) stopped German advance 30 miles from Paris, 'Race to the Sea' extended trench lines 450 miles, Battle of Tannenberg (August) - 170,000 Russian casualties
- ✅ **Phase 2 - Trench Warfare (1915-1917)**: Trench system (front line, support, reserve, No Man's Land 50-250 yards), conditions (mud, rats, lice, trench foot, shell shock), tactics (artillery barrage then 'over the top'), battles of attrition (Verdun 700,000 casualties, Somme 1 million, Passchendaele 500,000)
- ✅ **Phase 3 - Total War (1917-1918)**: Russian Revolution (1917) withdrew, US entry (April 1917) - unrestricted submarine warfare, Zimmermann Telegram, German Spring Offensive (March-July 1918) gained 40 miles but exhausted, Allied counteroffensive (August-November 1918) with 2 million US troops, Armistice (November 11, 1918, 11am)

#### 4. Major Battles (3 battles)
- ✅ **Verdun (February-December 1916)**: German strategy 'bleed France white', 10 months, 700,000 casualties (300,000 dead), French held Verdun, 'They shall not pass' (Pétain)
- ✅ **Somme (July-November 1916)**: British offensive, July 1 - 57,000 British casualties (19,000 dead) worst day in British military history, tanks used first time (September 1916), 1 million casualties, gained 6 miles
- ✅ **Passchendaele (July-November 1917)**: British offensive in Belgium, constant rain turned battlefield into mud, 500,000 casualties, gained 5 miles, 'Good God, did we really send men to fight in that?' (General Kiggell)

#### 5. New Weapons and Technology (7 types)
- ✅ Machine guns (Maxim 600 rounds/minute), artillery (70% of casualties), poison gas (1.3 million casualties, 90,000 deaths), tanks (British Mark I 1916), aircraft (reconnaissance, fighters, bombers, Red Baron shot down 80 planes), submarines (German U-boats, unrestricted submarine warfare), flamethrowers

#### 6. Home Front - Total War (7 aspects)
- ✅ Conscription (Britain 1916, US 1917), war economy (government controlled production, rationing), women's role (munitionettes, drove ambulances), propaganda (posters, films), censorship, financing (war bonds, taxes, inflation - Britain's debt increased 10x), social change (women gained vote, class barriers weakened)

#### 7. Treaty of Versailles (June 28, 1919)
- ✅ **Big Three**: Wilson (US - idealist), Clemenceau (France - revenge), Lloyd George (Britain - moderate)
- ✅ **Terms**: War Guilt Clause (Article 231), reparations £6.6 billion ($442 billion today), territorial losses (13% territory, Alsace-Lorraine to France, Polish Corridor to Poland, all colonies), military restrictions (army 100,000, no air force, no submarines, Rhineland demilitarized), League of Nations (US never joined)
- ✅ **Criticisms**: Too harsh (Germany humiliated, created resentment), too lenient (didn't permanently weaken Germany), hypocrisy (self-determination selective), ignored (Russia excluded, Germany not consulted), unenforceable (US didn't join League)

#### 8. Consequences (4 sections)
- ✅ **Casualties**: 10 million military deaths, 10 million civilian deaths, 21 million wounded, Spanish Flu (1918-1920) killed 50-100 million, lost generation (France lost 27% of men aged 18-27)
- ✅ **Political**: Empires collapsed (Russian, German, Austrian-Hungarian, Ottoman), new nations (Poland, Czechoslovakia, Yugoslavia), communism (Russian Revolution 1917), fascism (Italy 1922, Germany 1933), League of Nations failed
- ✅ **Economic**: Cost $200 billion ($3 trillion today), debt (Britain and France owed US $10 billion), inflation (Germany's hyperinflation 1923), Depression (1929), US became dominant economic power
- ✅ **Social**: Women's suffrage (Britain 1918, US 1920), class conflict, disillusionment ('lost generation' writers), pacifism ('never again'), trauma (shell shock/PTSD)

#### 9. Seeds of WWII (5 factors)
- ✅ Treaty of Versailles (German resentment, 'stab in the back' myth), economic crisis (reparations, hyperinflation, Great Depression), weak democracies (Weimar Germany, Italy turned to fascism), failed collective security (League couldn't stop aggression), unresolved issues (ethnic tensions, colonial grievances, arms race resumed)

#### 10. Historiographical Debates (4 debates)
- ✅ Who was responsible? (Fischer thesis vs Revisionist view)
- ✅ Could war have been avoided? (Determinist vs Contingency view)
- ✅ Was war futile? (Traditional 'lions led by donkeys' vs Revisionist view)
- ✅ Did war change society? (Optimist vs Pessimist view)

## 🎯 Civil Rights Movement (h2c3) - Comprehensive Enhancement

### Content Growth
- **Before**: 5 key points
- **After**: 24 key points
- **Growth**: +380% (19 new points)

### Major Additions

#### 1. Background (3 sections)
- ✅ **Reconstruction and Jim Crow (1865-1954)**: 13th Amendment (1865) abolished slavery, 14th Amendment (1868) citizenship, 15th Amendment (1870) voting rights, Reconstruction (1865-1877) enforced rights, End of Reconstruction (1877) federal troops withdrawn, Jim Crow laws (1880s-1950s) enforced segregation, Plessy v. Ferguson (1896) 'separate but equal' legalized segregation for 58 years
- ✅ **Segregation System**: Education (Black schools underfunded, received 1/3 funding), voting restrictions (poll taxes, literacy tests, grandfather clauses, white primaries), economic discrimination (low-wage jobs, denied bank loans), violence (3,500 lynched 1882-1968, KKK terrorism), social segregation (interracial marriage illegal)
- ✅ **Great Migration (1916-1970)**: 6 million Black Americans moved from South to North/West, push factors (Jim Crow, violence, poverty, boll weevil 1915), pull factors (industrial jobs, less racism, voting rights), destinations (Chicago, Detroit, New York, Los Angeles), impact (created Black urban communities, increased political power)

#### 2. Early Victories
- ✅ **Brown v. Board of Education (1954)**: Linda Brown (7 years old) had to walk 1 mile to segregated school, NAACP sued, Chief Justice Earl Warren unanimous decision (9-0) 'separate educational facilities are inherently unequal', overturned Plessy v. Ferguson, 'Brown II' (1955) ordered desegregation 'with all deliberate speed', resistance (Southern Manifesto 1956, massive resistance), Little Rock Nine (1957) - President Eisenhower sent federal troops

#### 3. Montgomery Bus Boycott (1955-1956)
- ✅ Background: Montgomery buses segregated, Blacks paid at front then re-entered at back
- ✅ Rosa Parks (December 1, 1955): 42-year-old seamstress, NAACP secretary, refused to give up seat, arrested
- ✅ Boycott: Black community (75% of bus riders) boycotted 381 days, walked or carpooled
- ✅ Leadership: MLK (26 years old) elected leader of Montgomery Improvement Association
- ✅ Tactics: Nonviolent resistance, mass meetings, carpool system (300 cars)
- ✅ Opposition: White Citizens' Council, KKK bombed King's house
- ✅ Victory: Browder v. Gayle (November 1956) - Supreme Court ruled bus segregation unconstitutional

#### 4. Key Figures (3 people)
- ✅ **Martin Luther King Jr. (1929-1968)**: Born Atlanta, PhD theology, philosophy (nonviolent resistance inspired by Gandhi, Christian love), strategy (mass protests, civil disobedience, media coverage), oratory (powerful speaker, biblical language), leadership (SCLC president 1957-1968), 'I Have a Dream' speech (August 28, 1963, 250,000 people), Nobel Peace Prize (1964, age 35), assassination (April 4, 1968, age 39)
- ✅ **Rosa Parks (1913-2005)**: Seamstress, NAACP secretary, trained at Highlander Folk School, arrested December 1, 1955 (not first - Claudette Colvin March 1955, but NAACP chose Parks as test case), myth vs reality (not 'tired old lady' - 42 years old, experienced activist), consequences (lost job, death threats, moved to Detroit 1957), legacy ('Mother of Civil Rights Movement')
- ✅ **Malcolm X (1925-1965)**: Born Malcolm Little, father killed by white supremacists (1931), imprisoned (1946-1952), Nation of Islam minister, advocated Black nationalism, self-defense ('by any means necessary'), contrast with King (rejected nonviolence, criticized King as 'Uncle Tom'), break with Nation of Islam (1964), pilgrimage to Mecca (saw Muslims of all races, softened views), assassination (February 21, 1965, age 39), legacy (inspired Black Power movement)

#### 5. Major Events (4 campaigns)
- ✅ **Birmingham Campaign (April-May 1963)**: Target ('most segregated city in America', Bull Connor), strategy (provoke violent response, gain media coverage), 'Letter from Birmingham Jail' (April 16, 1963), Children's Crusade (May 2-5, 1963) - 1,000+ children marched, police brutality (fire hoses, police dogs), victory (businesses desegregate), backlash (16th Street Baptist Church bombing September 15, 1963 - killed 4 Black girls ages 11-14)
- ✅ **March on Washington (August 28, 1963)**: Purpose (pressure Congress, demand jobs), organizers (A. Philip Randolph, Bayard Rustin, 'Big Six'), participants (250,000 people, 75% Black, 25% white, largest protest in US history), 'I Have a Dream' speech (improvised ending), impact (influenced Civil Rights Act 1964), criticism (Malcolm X called it 'Farce on Washington')
- ✅ **Freedom Summer (1964)**: Purpose (register Black voters in Mississippi - only 6.7% registered), organizers (SNCC, CORE, 1,000 white college students), Freedom Schools (taught Black history, civics), violence (1,000+ arrested, 80 beaten, 37 churches burned), Mississippi Burning murders (June 21, 1964) - 3 workers killed by KKK, MFDP challenged all-white delegation at Democratic Convention, impact (registered 17,000 voters, pressured Voting Rights Act)
- ✅ **Selma to Montgomery Marches (March 1965)**: Background (Selma 50% Black, only 2% registered), Bloody Sunday (March 7, 1965) - 600 marchers attacked by state troopers, 17 hospitalized, broadcast on national TV, Turnaround Tuesday (March 9), murder of James Reeb (March 9), Selma to Montgomery march (March 21-25) - 3,200 marchers, 25,000 joined at Montgomery, murder of Viola Liuzzo (March 25)

#### 6. Legislative Achievements (2 acts)
- ✅ **Civil Rights Act (1964)**: Proposed by Kennedy (June 1963), pushed by Johnson after Kennedy's assassination, Southern Democrats filibustered 75 days, passed July 2, 1964, provisions (Title II: banned segregation in public accommodations, Title IV: desegregate schools, Title VI: banned discrimination in federal programs, Title VII: banned employment discrimination, created EEOC), impact (ended legal segregation)
- ✅ **Voting Rights Act (1965)**: Johnson addressed Congress (March 15, 1965) after Bloody Sunday, used phrase 'We shall overcome', passed August 6, 1965, provisions (banned literacy tests, poll taxes, federal examiners could register voters, preclearance for states with discrimination history), impact (Black voter registration 43% to 62%, Black elected officials 100 to 10,500), Shelby County v. Holder (2013) struck down preclearance

#### 7. Black Power Movement (1966-1975)
- ✅ Origins: Stokely Carmichael coined 'Black Power' (June 1966)
- ✅ Philosophy: Black pride, self-determination, self-defense, rejected integration and nonviolence
- ✅ Black Panther Party (1966-1982): Founded by Huey Newton and Bobby Seale, armed patrols, free breakfast programs
- ✅ 1968 Olympics: Tommie Smith and John Carlos raised Black Power salute
- ✅ FBI repression: COINTELPRO, Fred Hampton killed (1969)

#### 8. Assassination of MLK (April 4, 1968)
- ✅ Context: In Memphis supporting sanitation workers' strike, focused on economic justice (Poor People's Campaign), opposed Vietnam War
- ✅ Assassination: Shot by James Earl Ray, age 39
- ✅ Riots: 125 cities, 43 dead, 3,500 injured, 27,000 arrested
- ✅ Legacy: Martyrdom elevated King to iconic status, movement fragmented
- ✅ Fair Housing Act (1968): Passed week after assassination

#### 9. Achievements and Limitations
- ✅ **Legal victories**: Ended legal segregation (1964), secured voting rights (1965), banned housing discrimination (1968)
- ✅ **Political power**: Black voter registration increased, 10,500 elected officials (2000), Obama elected president (2008)
- ✅ **Economic progress**: Black middle class grew, poverty rate fell 55% to 22%
- ✅ **Limitations**: Economic inequality persisted (median Black wealth 1/10 of white), residential segregation continued, mass incarceration (1 in 3 Black men imprisoned in lifetime), police brutality continued

#### 10. Long-Term Impact and Historiographical Debates
- ✅ **Impact**: Inspired movements (women's rights, LGBTQ+, disability, immigrant), global influence (anti-apartheid), changed America (interracial marriage legal 1967, integrated schools/workplaces), backlash (Southern strategy, war on drugs, mass incarceration), ongoing struggle (Black Lives Matter 2013-present)
- ✅ **Debates**: Nonviolence vs militancy, role of federal government, top-down vs bottom-up, success or failure, class vs race
