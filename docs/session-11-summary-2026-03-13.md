- Dopamine (reward, motivation, Parkinson's disease, schizophrenia)

- Central Nervous System (brain, spinal cord)
- Peripheral Nervous System (somatic, autonomic)
- Sympathetic vs. Parasympathetic (fight-or-flight vs. rest-and-digest)

**Brain Structure**:
- Cerebral cortex (frontal, parietal, temporal, occipital lobes)

- Hypothalamus & thalamus (homeostasis, sensory relay)
- Brain stem (medulla, pons, reticular formation, cerebellum)

**Endocrine System**:
- Hormones vs. nervous system (slower, longer-lasting, widespread)
- Key hormones (adrenaline, cortisol, testosterone)

**Biological Explanations of Aggression**:
- Genetic factors (twin studies, MAOA 'warrior gene', Brunner syndrome)
- Neural factors (amygdala overactivity, prefrontal cortex underactivity, low serotonin)
- Hormones (testosterone, cortisol)

**Circadian Rhythms**:
- Suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN) as master clock
- Melatonin regulation
- Disruption effects (shift work, jet lag, SAD)

**Brain Plasticity**:
- Types (developmental, adult, recovery)
- Mechanisms (neurogenesis, synaptic pruning, dendritic branching)

**Case Studies**:

**Research Methods**:

- Twin studies

**Evaluation**:

### 2. Learning Theories (psy2c2)

**Classical Conditioning**:
- Definition and key terms (UCS, UCR, NS, CS, CR)
- Pavlov's dogs experiment (1897)
- Principles (acquisition, extinction, spontaneous recovery, generalization, discrimination, timing)
- Watson & Little Albert (1920) - ethical issues
- Applications (phobia treatment, advertising, taste aversion)

**Operant Conditioning**:
- Definition and Law of Effect (Thorndike)
- Skinner box experiments
- Shaping (successive approximations)
- Reinforcement types:
  - Positive reinforcement (add pleasant stimulus)
  - Negative reinforcement (remove unpleasant stimulus)
- Punishment types:
  - Positive punishment (add unpleasant stimulus)
  - Negative punishment (remove pleasant stimulus)

**Reinforcement Schedules**:
- Continuous vs. partial reinforcement
- Partial reinforcement effect
- Ratio schedules:
  - Fixed ratio (FR) - high rate, pause after reinforcement
  - Variable ratio (VR) - highest rate, most resistant to extinction
- Interval schedules:
  - Fixed interval (FI) - scalloped response pattern
  - Variable interval (VI) - steady, moderate rate

**Applications**:
- Education (token economy, praise, grades)
- Parenting (time-out, reward charts)
- Animal training (clicker training, shaping)
- Workplace (bonuses, commissions)
- Behavior modification (ABA for autism)

**Social Learning Theory (Bandura)**:
- Cognitive factors (attention, retention, reproduction, motivation)
- Reciprocal determinism
- Bobo doll experiment (1961)
- Vicarious reinforcement
- Applications (media effects, modeling in therapy, education, advertising)

**Evaluation**:
- Classical conditioning (strengths: scientific, applications; limitations: reductionist, animal research, deterministic, biological preparedness)
- Operant conditioning (strengths: scientific, wide applications; limitations: reductionist, ethical issues, individual differences, punishment problems)
- Social learning theory (strengths: cognitive factors, explains complex learning, real-world relevance; limitations: correlation ≠ causation, individual differences, underestimates biology, lab studies)

**Comparison**:
- Classical (involuntary, association, passive)
- Operant (voluntary, consequences, active)
- Social (complex, observation, cognitive)

### 3. Developmental Psychology (psy2c3)

**Piaget's Cognitive Development Theory**:
- Overview (children think differently, stages, active construction)
- Mechanisms (schema, assimilation, accommodation, equilibration)

**Piaget's Stages**:
1. **Sensorimotor (0-2 years)**:
   - Learn through senses and motor actions
   - Object permanence (8-12 months)
   - A-not-B error

2. **Preoperational (2-7 years)**:
   - Language development, symbolic thinking
   - Egocentrism (Three Mountains Task)
   - Conservation failure (number, liquid, mass)
   - Centration, irreversibility, animism

3. **Concrete Operational (7-11 years)**:
   - Logical thinking about concrete objects
   - Conservation achieved
   - Classification, seriation, transitivity
   - Cannot think abstractly

4. **Formal Operational (11+ years)**:
   - Abstract thinking
   - Hypothetical-deductive reasoning (pendulum task)
   - Metacognition

**Evaluation of Piaget**:
- Strengths (pioneering, influential, cross-cultural support)
- Limitations (underestimated young children, overestimated adolescents, stage theory too rigid, ignored social/cultural factors, small sample)

**Vygotsky's Sociocultural Theory**:
- Social interaction drives development
- Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD)
- Scaffolding
- Difference from Piaget (social vs. individual)

**Erikson's Psychosocial Stages**:
- 8 stages across lifespan
- Stage 1 (0-1): Trust vs. Mistrust
- Stage 2 (1-3): Autonomy vs. Shame/Doubt
- Stage 3 (3-6): Initiative vs. Guilt
- Stage 4 (6-12): Industry vs. Inferiority
- Stage 5 (12-18): Identity vs. Role Confusion (identity statuses)
- Stage 6 (18-40): Intimacy vs. Isolation
- Stage 7 (40-65): Generativity vs. Stagnation
- Stage 8 (65+): Integrity vs. Despair

**Attachment Theory**:
- Bowlby's theory (innate, evolutionary, critical period, monotropy, internal working model, maternal deprivation hypothesis)
- Ainsworth's Strange Situation (1970) - procedure and observations

**Attachment Types**:
- Secure (60-70%) - sensitive caregiver, confident outcome
- Insecure-avoidant (20-25%) - rejecting caregiver, emotionally distant outcome
- Insecure-resistant/ambivalent (10-15%) - inconsistent caregiver, anxious outcome
- Disorganized (5-10%) - frightening caregiver, highest risk

**Evaluation of Strange Situation**:
- Strengths (standardized, predictive validity, cross-cultural)
- Limitations (culture bias, mother-only, artificial, temperament, snapshot)

**Deprivation vs. Privation**:
- Deprivation (loss after attachment formed) - protest, despair, detachment, reversible
- Privation (never forming attachment) - more severe, permanent effects

**Romanian Orphanage Studies (Rutter)**:
- Adopted before 6 months: normal development
- Adopted after 6 months: some lasting effects
- Adopted after 2 years: significant problems
- Supports critical period, shows some recovery possible

**Case Study: Genie (1970)**:
- Extreme privation (locked in room since 20 months)
- Never acquired grammar, could not form attachments
- Supports critical period for language and attachment
- Limitations (one case, possible brain damage, ethical issues)

**Effects of Day Care**:
- High-quality: no negative effects
- Low-quality: may increase aggression
- Quality factors (low ratio, trained staff, stable staff, stimulating environment)
- NICHD Study: quality matters more than quantity, family factors most important

**Cultural Variations**:
- Individualist cultures (US, UK, Germany) - value independence
- Collectivist cultures (Japan, Israel) - value interdependence
- Strange Situation may not be valid across cultures

**Adolescent Development**:
- Physical changes (puberty, early vs. late maturation)
- Cognitive changes (formal operations, imaginary audience, personal fable)
- Social development (identity formation, peer relationships, parent-child conflict)

**Aging & Late Adulthood**:
- Cognitive changes (fluid intelligence declines, crystallized intelligence stable)
- Dementia (Alzheimer's disease)
- Social changes (retirement, grandparenting, widowhood)
- Successful aging factors

**Evaluation of Developmental Theories**:
- Piaget, Vygotsky, Erikson, Bowlby/Ainsworth - strengths and limitations
